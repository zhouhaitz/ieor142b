Goal: This project is intended as a learning experience where you apply the deep learning foundations and methodologies covered in class to a real dataset or problem. You will work in groups of 4 to 6 students*, no more and no less. You will submit a report and give a short team presentation on your work.
Description:
Throughout the course, we have studied the theoretical underpinnings and practical implementations of modern deep learning architectures. In this project, you will procure a dataset in a domain of interest and apply deep learning methods from the course to perform a learning task on this data. We have seen applications across different domains in lecture and homeworks, and I encourage you to pursue an application you care about or something that could serve as the foundation for a useful tool.
Grading is holistic. A conceptually interesting approach can receive full credit even if the final model performance is modest. This applies equally if you curate your own dataset through web scraping or manual collection. Approach this project not just as a course requirement, but as an opportunity to build a portfolio piece that demonstrates what you have learned in this course.
Grading: Grading will be done semi-holistically. While there is no rigid rubric, we will evaluate your project across the following dimensions based on how appropriately you apply deep learning methodologies:
Project description and motivation: Is there a clearly identified, well-described use-case? Is the application well-motivated within its specific domain?
Data and learning problem: Is the dataset clearly explored and main problem you are trying to learn/solve well formulated? E.g. if your problem falls under supervised learning, you should make very clear what the features and labels are and what mapping you are trying to learn between them. Discuss any data quality issues, class imbalances, or necessary preprocessing steps that may be relevant.
Methodology: Is the chosen network architecture and approach appropriate for the problem at hand? You should detail your choice of layers you use to build your model (e.g., convolutional filters, pooling layers, or multi-head self-attention). Discuss your selections for activation functions (such as ReLU for hidden layers and Softmax for multiclass output) and how you establish the objective function:
\min_{\theta} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} L(f_{\theta}(X_{i}), Y_{i}) + R(\theta)
Justify your choice of loss L, optimizer (e.g., Minibatch SGD vs. Adam), and regularization strategy R(\theta) (e.g., L_2 penalties, Dropout, or Early Stopping). Further, you must carefully describe and implement at least one advanced part of methodology that we did not cover in class. There are many, many variations to architectures, optimization procedures, activation functions and so on that can give improvements in some settings (you may use any resources for discovering these, but as with the mini-project one recommendation is browsing papers in the Journal of Machine Learning Research, NeurIPS, and ICML ).
Discussion of results: Are training and validation loss curves provided and analyzed as diagnostic tools? You should interpret these curves to diagnose whether your model is underfitting (high bias) or overfitting (high variance). Discuss the final metrics and whether the methodology offers a practical improvement for the described use-case. A conclusion of "we attempted this architecture, but due to spatial structure issues or data constraints, the performance is insufficient for production" is a completely valid scientific result if well-justified.
Discussion of safety, security, and ethics: Suppose that your model was deployed at a large scale, e.g. for a large business or governmental organization. You must provide a thoughtful discussion of any potential safety, security, and ethics concerns surrounding your method and the problem you are working on. Note that due to limited possible scope of the project, it is very possible that your model would be problematic to deploy (e.g. if it is working in a medical context and is unable to reach very high accuracy / low type II error, that may be problematic). The purpose of this section is to think about and discuss any potential such issues. For this, we suggest to have a thorough discussion with your team and then synthesize your thoughts as this section of your report. You are also encouraged to refer to the lab sessions from April 3 and April 10 where we discussed these topics. 
Presentation: You will record a short (~10 minute) presentation discussing your project. This should provide a high-level overview of your problem formulation, network design, and results.
Each category will be ranked as "insufficient, lacking, satisfactory, great, exceptional". A project deemed satisfactory in all aspects receives a 80% mark. Deviations alter the score by 5% (e.g., "great" is +5%, "exceptional" is +10%, "lacking" is -5%, and "insufficient" is -10%), capped at 100%.
Bonus points (up to +5% bonus, past 100% possible): Deploy your model or build an interactive tool (e.g., using Plotly/Dash, HuggingFace Spaces, Streamlit, Vercel, …) that allows others to interact with your mode in a meaningful way. Include a link to the tool or a demo recording in your report. You will receive +2.5% for a great rating and +5% for an exceptional rating.
Report format: Your project report should be 4 to 5 pages, excluding the appendix. You should include an abstract that provides a very brief summary of your project and main results. Include your code in an appendix. Additional figures that do not fit in the main text may also be placed in the appendix, provided they are referenced in the report.
